# Methodology

A building block of the [[Thesis Journey]]: choosing and structuring the research approach. The methodology defines how the student will answer their research question -- what data to collect, how to analyze it, and what claims can be made from the results.

## Common Approaches

- **Qualitative** -- interviews, case studies, grounded theory. Requires [[Interview Partners]] and careful coding.
- **Quantitative** -- surveys, statistical analysis, experiments. Requires [[Data Access]] and sufficient sample sizes.
- **Mixed methods** -- combining qualitative and quantitative approaches for richer results.
- **Design science** -- building and evaluating an artifact (common in information systems and engineering).
- **Literature-based** -- systematic reviews, meta-analyses. Relies heavily on [[Literature]] quality and coverage.

## When It Is Decided

Methodology is typically defined during the [[Planning Stage]] in close collaboration with [[Supervisors]]. The choice is shaped by the research question, available data, and the student's skills. It is one of the areas where [[Academic Governance]] is strongest -- the supervisor must approve the methodological approach.

## Pain Points

Students often underestimate the scope implied by their chosen methodology. A "quick survey" turns into months of respondent recruitment. "A few interviews" becomes a logistics nightmare. Without benchmarks or templates, students lack calibration on what is realistic.

## Where AI Could Help

AI could suggest methodologies based on the research question and available [[Data Access]], flag scope risks early, and provide templates or reference examples from similar theses. This would be most valuable during [[Planning Stage]].
