# Absorptive Capacity

Cohen & Levinthal (1990) defined absorptive capacity as a firm's ability to recognize the value of external knowledge, assimilate it, and exploit it commercially. This concept is central to understanding why thesis collaborations deliver value even without immediate product outputs.

## The Mechanism

Firms must build internal capacity before they can effectively use external knowledge. Cognitive proximity to [[Universities]] significantly increases the likelihood of translating academic knowledge into innovation. Without absorptive capacity, a firm may encounter valuable research but lack the organizational structures to recognize or act on it. Thesis collaborations build this capacity incrementally.

## The Hidden Effect

Alpaydin & Fitjar (2024) found that even when thesis collaborations do not immediately result in products, they significantly increase firms' cognitive and organizational proximity to [[Universities]] -- a factor empirically linked to higher future innovation probability. This is the hidden effect of [[University-Industry Collaboration Research|UIC]]: the collaboration itself upgrades the firm's capacity to innovate, independent of the specific project outcome.

## How Thesis Collaborations Build It

Through a thesis project on [[Studyond]], a company gains: exposure to academic methods and frameworks (cognitive proximity), structured interaction with [[Supervisors]] and [[Researchers]] (organizational proximity), alignment with university processes and norms (institutional proximity), and personal relationships with [[Students]] and faculty (social proximity). Each collaboration deepens these proximities, making subsequent collaborations more productive.

## Path Dependency

Innovation investment is path-dependent (Bishop et al., 2011; Perkmann et al., 2021). Firms that invest early in university relationships extract more value later. Experienced collaborators systematically outperform first-time participants. Delayed entry creates a compounding disadvantage that is difficult to recover from.
