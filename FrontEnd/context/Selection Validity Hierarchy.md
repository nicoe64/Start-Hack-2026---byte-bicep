# Selection Validity Hierarchy

Schmidt & Hunter (1998) meta-analysis establishing the predictive validity hierarchy for personnel selection methods and job performance prediction. This is the foundational evidence for [[Studyond]]'s talent value proposition.

## The Hierarchy

| Selection Method | Validity (r) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| [[Work Sample Tests]] | .54 | Strongest single predictor |
| Structured interviews | .51 | Close second, shorter observation window |
| Cognitive ability tests (GMA) | .51 | Strong but controversial; bias concerns |
| Job knowledge tests | .48 | Requires domain-specific testing |
| Integrity tests | .41 | Useful supplement |
| Unstructured interviews | .38 | Widely used despite lower validity |
| Assessment centers | .37 | Expensive, time-intensive |
| CVs / resumes | .18-.26 | Poor predictor, especially early career |
| Years of experience | .18 | Diminishing returns after ~5 years |
| Reference checks | .26 | Unreliable signal |

## Key Insight

[[Work Sample Tests]] -- evaluating candidates through actual job-relevant work over time -- are the single strongest predictor of future job performance. [[Thesis as Work Sample|Thesis projects qualify as natural work samples]]: long-form, skill-based, observed in real conditions, with dual assessment from academic [[Supervisors]] and company mentors.

## Why CVs Fail for Early Career

CVs and resumes have a validity of only .18-.26, making them among the weakest predictors. This problem is compounded by [[CV Signal Degradation]] from GenAI inflation and the structural reality that early-career candidates have minimal work history to differentiate themselves. The 7-8 seconds recruiters spend on initial CV review (The Ladders, 2018) further reduces signal quality.

## Implications for Studyond

This hierarchy is the scientific backbone of the [[Company Value Proposition - Talent]]. By enabling [[Companies]] to evaluate candidates through 4-6 month thesis projects, [[Studyond]] moves hiring from the weakest predictor (CVs) to the strongest ([[Work Sample Tests]]). See [[Skills-Based Hiring]] for the broader macro trend.

## Source

Schmidt, F. L., & Hunter, J. E. (1998). *The validity and utility of selection methods in personnel psychology.* Psychological Bulletin, 124(2), 262-274.
