# CV Signal Degradation

GenAI inflates CV surface quality without improving underlying capability, making candidates look more similar, not more distinguishable. Combined with structural weaknesses in early-career signaling, CVs are becoming less reliable as a screening tool.

## GenAI Inflation

Harvard Business Review (2023) documented how generative AI tools allow candidates to produce polished CVs, cover letters, and portfolio pieces with minimal effort. This inflates surface quality across the board. The problem is acute at the early-career level, where candidates have less work history to create genuine differentiation. When every CV looks equally polished, the signal collapses.

## The 7-Second Problem

Recruiters spend 7-8 seconds on initial CV review (The Ladders, 2018). This was already insufficient for meaningful evaluation. With GenAI-inflated CVs, the brief scan yields even less useful information. The recruiter sees polish, not capability.

## Early-Career Signal Gap

[[Students]] are invisible on professional networks until weeks before graduation (LinkedIn Economic Graph, 2022). Graduates have limited work history, making CV-based assessment structurally weak. Degree prestige is a proxy, not a performance predictor. Traditional recruitment channels like career fairs and job boards provide brief, shallow interactions. The combined effect: [[Companies]] that rely on CVs for early-career hiring are screening on noise, not signal.

## Implications

This strengthens the case for [[Work Sample Tests]] and [[Thesis as Work Sample]]. [[Companies]] that engage [[Students]] during their thesis period evaluate candidates through actual work before competitors even know they exist. The [[Selection Validity Hierarchy]] already showed CVs as weak predictors (.18-.26). GenAI inflation makes a bad signal worse. [[Skills-Based Hiring]] is the structural response.
