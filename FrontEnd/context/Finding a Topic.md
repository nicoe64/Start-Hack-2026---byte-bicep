# Finding a Topic

The first major decision in the [[Thesis Journey]]: choosing a research question or problem to investigate. The topic anchors everything that follows -- it determines which [[Supervisors]] are relevant, which [[Companies]] might partner, and what [[Methodology]] is appropriate. Getting it right matters enormously; getting it wrong costs months.

## Traditional Approaches

- **Google** -- broad, unstructured, yields generic inspiration at best.
- **Professor suggestions** -- limited to what one supervisor has in mind.
- **University bulletin boards** -- small inventory, often outdated.
- **Personal network** -- depends entirely on who you know.
- **Company websites** -- scattered, inconsistent, hard to compare.

All of these channels are fragmented, low-signal, and offer no personalization. Students waste weeks in an unstructured search.

## How Studyond Transforms This

[[Studyond]] aggregates 7,500+ curated [[Topics]] from 185+ [[Companies]], all browsable and filterable by [[Fields]], degree level, and study program. The [[Matching Engine]] suggests relevant topics based on the student's profile, interests, and academic background. The [[Student AI Agent]] helps students articulate what they are looking for through dialogue rather than keyword search.

**Time to first value:** a student can browse matching topics within minutes of signup, compared to weeks of manual searching through traditional channels.

## Why It Matters

Finding a topic is the gateway action. Without it, no thesis starts. This is why [[Studyond]] focused here first -- it is the highest-leverage point in the [[Thesis Journey]] for reducing student friction and connecting the [[Three-Sided Marketplace]].
