# Literature

A building block of the [[Thesis Journey]]: finding, reading, and managing academic sources. The literature review grounds the thesis in existing research, identifies gaps, and establishes the theoretical framework. It is relevant across all stages but demands the most attention during [[Planning Stage]] and [[Writing and Finalization]].

## The Process

Students must identify relevant papers, books, and reports; read and synthesize them; organize references; and write a coherent literature review that positions their research within the field. This is iterative -- new findings during [[Execution Stage]] often send students back to the literature.

## Pain Points

- **Discovery** -- knowing what to search for and where. Google Scholar and university databases return overwhelming results with no personalization.
- **Depth calibration** -- how many sources are enough? Students either read too little (weak foundation) or too much (analysis paralysis).
- **Currency** -- staying up to date with new publications during a months-long thesis process.
- **Management** -- organizing references, maintaining citation consistency, and avoiding accidental plagiarism.

## Where AI Could Help

- Surface relevant papers based on the thesis topic, [[Methodology]], and research question.
- Suggest seminal works and key authors in the field.
- Summarize papers to help students triage what to read deeply.
- Track new publications that become relevant as the thesis evolves.
- Assist with citation formatting during [[Writing and Finalization]].
