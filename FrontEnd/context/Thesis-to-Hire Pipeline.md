# Thesis-to-Hire Pipeline

Consolidated evidence for the thesis-to-hiring pathway -- [[Studyond]]'s most distinctive value mechanic. A thesis project functions as a 4-6 month [[Work Sample Tests|work-sample]] evaluation, creating a natural pathway from academic collaboration to employment.

## The Core Argument

Traditional hiring: CV, then interview (1-2 hours), then offer, then hope for the best. Thesis-to-hire: topic, then thesis (4-6 months), then observed work, then informed decision. The thesis gives [[Companies]] 4-6 months of observed, real-conditions work before making a hiring decision -- orders of magnitude more signal than any interview.

## Evidence Layer 1: Selection Science

[[Work Sample Tests]] are the strongest performance predictor (r=.54) per the [[Selection Validity Hierarchy]] (Schmidt & Hunter, 1998). A thesis meets all work-sample criteria: job-relevant tasks, observable over time, real conditions, multiple competencies, structured evaluation. This makes the pipeline structurally superior to CV-based hiring.

## Evidence Layer 2: Intern-to-Hire Conversion

NACE (National Association of Colleges and Employers) data validates internship-to-hire conversion pathways. A thesis is a longer, deeper engagement than a typical internship, providing even more signal for both parties. The conversion is mutual -- both the company and the student have enough information to make an informed decision about fit.

## Dual-Value Mechanism

The pipeline delivers value on two paths simultaneously. **Innovation path:** the thesis generates evidence, validates hypotheses, and builds [[Absorptive Capacity]]. **Talent path:** the thesis evaluates candidates through observed work, leading to informed hiring. Even when a thesis does not lead to a hire, it delivers innovation value per [[University-Industry Collaboration Research]].
